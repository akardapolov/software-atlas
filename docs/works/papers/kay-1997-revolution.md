# The Computer Revolution Hasn't Happened Yet

| | |
|---|---|
| **Author** | Alan Kay |
| **Year** | 1997 |
| **Publication** | OOPSLA Keynote |
| **Topic(s)** | Personal computing, education, software industry |

## Summary

In this provocative OOPSLA keynote address, Alan Kay argues that **despite decades of progress, the computer revolution has not yet happened**. He criticizes the software industry for focusing on technology rather than human potential, and calls for a shift toward empowering creativity and learning.

## Key Ideas

### What Revolution Would Look Like

Kay describes a true computer revolution as:

- **Computers as amplifiers of human intellect**
- **Focus on creativity and learning**, not productivity
- **Literacy in computation** — programming as a fundamental skill
- **Collaboration and sharing** — not just individual productivity

He contrasts this with:
- **Current state** — computers for document editing, spreadsheets
- **Business productivity** — automation of existing workflows
- **Incremental improvements** — not paradigmatic shifts

### Problems with Current Software

Kay identifies key issues:

- **Complexity over comprehension** — software is too hard to understand
- **Cargo cult programming** — copying patterns without understanding
- **Tooling over thinking** — IDE features don't fix bad design
- **Training gap** — programmers learn tools, not fundamentals

### Education Focus

Kay emphasizes that the revolution requires:

- **Teaching programming to everyone** — not just CS students
- **Focus on thinking** — problem-solving, not syntax
- **Building mental models** — understanding systems deeply
- **Creative expression** — computing as art and science

## Historical Significance

### Critique of Industry

The keynote was a wake-up call:

- **Reflection on progress** — have we truly advanced?
- **Critique of trends** — complexity, frameworks over fundamentals
- **Call to action** — what should we really be building?

### Influence on Education and Research

Kay's ideas influenced:

- **Computational thinking** — programming as literacy movement
- **Research on creativity support** - tools for thinking and learning
- **Simpler systems** - Smalltalk, Squeak as exemplars

## Legacy

The keynote remains relevant:

- **Criticism of over-engineering** — complexity for its own sake
- **Human-centered computing** - technology amplifying human potential
- **Software craftsmanship** - focus on understanding and simplicity
