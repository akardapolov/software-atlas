## Biography

Alan Curtis Kay is an American computer scientist known for pioneering
work in object-oriented programming, personal computing, and graphical
user interfaces. At Xerox PARC in the 1970s, he led the group that
created **Smalltalk** — the language that defined what OOP means to
most programmers.

Kay envisioned the **Dynabook** (1968) — a personal computer for children
of all ages, portable and interactive — decades before tablets and laptops
existed. This vision drove much of his work at PARC, including Smalltalk,
overlapping windows, and graphical user interface concepts later adopted
by Apple (Lisa, Macintosh) and Microsoft (Windows).

Kay received the Turing Award in 2003 "for pioneering many of the ideas
at the root of contemporary object-oriented programming languages."

## Key Contributions

### Smalltalk (1972)

Smalltalk was the first language to fully embrace the idea that
**everything is an object**, and objects communicate via **messages**.
Key innovations:

- **Message passing** as the fundamental mechanism (not method calls)
- **Late binding** — the receiver decides how to handle a message
- **Live environment** — edit code while the system is running
- **Integrated IDE** — browser, debugger, inspector in one environment
- **MVC pattern** — Model-View-Controller originated in Smalltalk

### "OOP Is About Messaging"

Kay has consistently emphasised that the **big idea** of OOP is
not classes, inheritance, or encapsulation — it is **messaging**:

> "I thought of objects being like biological cells and/or individual
> computers on a network, only able to communicate with messages."

This insight separates Kay's original vision of OOP from what most
mainstream languages (C++, Java) implemented. His vision is closer to:

- Erlang's actor model (isolated processes communicating via messages)
- Microservices (independent services communicating via APIs)
- Alan Kay's OOP ≠ C++/Java's OOP

### Dynabook Concept (1968)

A vision for a portable personal computer for education. It influenced:

- Apple's iPad and laptop designs
- One Laptop per Child (OLPC) project
- The entire concept of personal computing

### Influence on GUI and Personal Computing

At Xerox PARC, Kay's group developed:

- Overlapping windows
- Pop-up menus
- The desktop metaphor (with others at PARC)

Steve Jobs saw a demo in 1979, which directly led to Apple Lisa
and Macintosh.

## Influence

### Influenced by

- **Ivan Sutherland** — Sketchpad (1963): graphical interaction, object instances
- **Seymour Papert** — Logo, constructionist education philosophy
- **Doug Engelbart** — "The Mother of All Demos" (1968): interactive computing
- **Simula (Dahl & Nygaard)** — classes and objects concept

### Influenced

- **GoF** — Design Patterns emerged from the Smalltalk community
- **Joe Armstrong** — Erlang's process model echoes Kay's "messaging" OOP
- **Yukihiro Matsumoto (Matz)** — Ruby's design strongly influenced by Smalltalk
- **Brad Cox** — Objective-C combined C with Smalltalk-like messaging
- **All GUI systems** — windowing, menus, direct manipulation

## Quotes

> "The best way to predict the future is to invent it."

> "OOP to me means only messaging, local retention and protection
> and hiding of state-process, and extreme late-binding of all things."

> "Most software today is very much like an Egyptian pyramid with
> millions of bricks piled on top of each other, with no structural
> integrity, but just done by brute force and thousands of slaves."

> "The computer revolution hasn't happened yet."
