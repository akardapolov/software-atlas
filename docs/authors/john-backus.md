## Biography

John Backus was an American computer scientist whose career spans two very
different eras of programming. In the 1950s, he led the IBM team that created
**FORTRAN**, the first widely successful high-level programming language.
Decades later, he became one of the strongest critics of the dominant
imperative style that FORTRAN helped establish.

Backus worked at IBM during the formative years of commercial computing.
His work on high-level languages and compilers showed that programmers did
not need to write everything in assembly language to achieve performance.

In 1977, he received the Turing Award. His Turing lecture, later published
in 1978 as **"Can Programming Be Liberated from the von Neumann Style?"**,
became one of the foundational texts of functional programming.

## Key Contributions

### FORTRAN (1957)

Backus led the team that created **FORTRAN** (FORmula TRANslation), the
first high-level language to achieve broad industrial success.

Its importance is hard to overstate:

- Proved that high-level languages could be efficient
- Made programming accessible beyond machine specialists
- Helped establish compiler construction as a major field
- Created the model for many later languages

FORTRAN changed programming from machine control to symbolic expression.

### Compiler Engineering

The FORTRAN compiler was itself a breakthrough. Many engineers initially
believed high-level languages would always be too slow. Backus's team proved
otherwise through aggressive optimisation techniques.

This was one of the moments when software became an engineering discipline
in its own right.

### Backus Notation / BNF Lineage

Backus introduced a formal notation for describing programming language
syntax, later refined and popularised with Peter Naur as **Backus–Naur Form (BNF)**.

This gave language designers a precise way to specify grammars and strongly
influenced compiler design, parser construction, and language standardisation.

### The Functional Programming Manifesto (1978)

In his Turing lecture, Backus argued that conventional programming is trapped
by the **von Neumann style**:

- State updates
- Word-at-a-time thinking
- Low-level sequencing
- Excessive complexity in reasoning

He proposed a more mathematical approach based on function composition and
algebraic reasoning. This was one of the clearest early calls for functional
programming as an alternative design tradition.

## Influence

### Influenced by

- **John von Neumann** — the architecture and style Backus later criticised
- **Alonzo Church** — functional abstraction as an alternative computational model

### Influenced

- **John Hughes** — modularity and composition in FP
- **Rich Hickey** — critique of incidental complexity
- **Functional programming as a movement** — especially typed FP and compositional design

## Why Backus Is So Important

Backus is historically unusual because he shaped both sides of the story:

- He helped make imperative programming mainstream through FORTRAN
- He later provided one of the most important critiques of imperative style

That gives his FP manifesto unusual weight: it is not a rejection from the
outside, but a self-critique from one of the builders of the mainstream.
