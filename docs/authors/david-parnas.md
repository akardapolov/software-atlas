## Biography

David Lorge Parnas is a computer scientist and software engineer widely regarded as one of the founders of software architecture and modular design. Born in 1941 in Plattsburgh, New York, he studied electrical engineering at Carnegie Mellon University, later completing a PhD in electrical engineering there. He spent most of his career at Bell Labs and later at Carnegie Mellon University as a professor.

Parnas made foundational contributions in 1972 with his seminal paper "On the Criteria To Be Used in Decomposing Systems into Modules." This work introduced the concept of **information hiding** — modules should hide internal design decisions that are likely to change. A good module boundary protects the rest of the system from internal changes.

He argued that decomposition is not about splitting work into pieces — it's about managing change. Parnas proposed distinguishing between **secrets** (implementation details) and **information** (interface/external behavior) that a module should expose to hide from external dependencies and protect from internal changes.

## Influence

### Influenced by

- **Edsger Dijkstra** — Parnas's modular thinking extended Dijkstra's structured programming principles
- **Fred Brooks** — referenced Parnas's ideas on information hiding in "The Mythical Man-Month"

### Influenced

- **Bertrand Meyer** — Parnas's information hiding influenced Meyer's design by contract thinking
- **Eric Evans** — Parnas's modular decomposition is a foundational idea for Domain-Driven Design

- **Modern software architecture** — layered and hexagonal architectures directly build on Parnas's modular foundation
- **ALGOL community** — Parnas is widely cited in modular design literature
