## Biography

Kurt Gödel was an Austrian-American logician, mathematician, and philosopher, considered one of the most significant logicians in history. He made fundamental contributions to mathematical logic and philosophy of mathematics.

Gödel spent most of his career at the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, New Jersey, where he became close friends with Albert Einstein. He suffered from mental health issues throughout his life, including paranoia and a fear of being poisoned. He died in 1978 after refusing to eat due to his belief that his food was being poisoned.

## Key Contributions

### Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems (1931)

Gödel's incompleteness theorems are two theorems of mathematical logic that demonstrate the inherent limitations of formal axiomatic systems.

**First Incompleteness Theorem:** Any consistent formal system F within which a certain amount of elementary arithmetic can be carried out is incomplete; i.e., there are statements of the language of F which can neither be proved nor disproved in F.

**Second Incompleteness Theorem:** For any consistent system F within which a certain amount of elementary arithmetic can be carried out, the consistency of F cannot be proved in F itself.

This was a devastating blow to David Hilbert's program, which sought to prove the consistency and completeness of mathematics using finitary methods.

### Gödel Numbering

Gödel developed a method for encoding logical formulas and proofs as natural numbers. Each symbol in a formal language is assigned a unique number, and each formula is encoded as a sequence of these numbers using prime factorisation.

This technique allowed Gödel to construct self-referential statements (similar to "this statement is false") within formal systems, which was key to his incompleteness proofs. Gödel numbering is the foundation of modern computability theory and computer science.

### Completeness Theorem (1929)

Before his incompleteness theorems, Gödel proved his **completeness theorem**: first-order logic is *complete* — every logically valid formula can be derived from the axioms. This established first-order logic as a sound and complete logical system.

The contrast between Gödel's completeness (1929) and incompleteness (1931) results is profound:
- **First-order logic**: Complete but not expressive enough for all of arithmetic
- **Arithmetic (Peano axioms)**: Expressive but incomplete

## Influence

### Influenced by

- **David Hilbert** — Hilbert's program provided the target for Gödel's incompleteness theorems
- **Gottlob Frege** — Frege's formal logic provided the foundation for Gödel's work
- **Cantor** — Set theory and transfinite numbers

### Influenced

- **Alonzo Church** — Gödel's work on formal systems and incompleteness directly influenced Church's lambda calculus and the Church-Turing thesis
- **Alan Turing** — Turing's work on computability builds directly on Gödel's insights about formal systems
- **Modern computer science** — Gödel numbering is the foundation of encoding information; the limits Gödel discovered are fundamental to what computers can and cannot do
- **Theoretical computer science** — Computability theory, complexity theory, and formal verification all trace back to Gödel's work
