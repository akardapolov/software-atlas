## Biography

Alan Turing was a British mathematician and computer scientist who is widely considered the father of theoretical computer science and artificial intelligence. During World War II, he worked at Bletchley Park, Britain's codebreaking centre, where he devised techniques for speeding up the breaking of German ciphers, including improvements to the pre-war Polish bombe method.

After the war, Turing worked at the National Physical Laboratory, where he designed the Automatic Computing Engine (ACE), one of the first designs for a stored-program computer. He later joined the University of Manchester, where he helped develop the Manchester computers and became interested in mathematical biology.

## Key Contributions

### Turing Machine (1936)

Turing introduced the concept of an abstract machine that could read and write symbols on an infinitely long tape following a set of rules. This simple model formalised the intuitive notion of "algorithm" and provided a mathematical definition of computation.

Key aspects:
- **Infinite tape** divided into cells, each containing a symbol
- **Read/write head** that can move left or right
- **State register** storing the current state
- **Transition function** dictating what to write, which direction to move, and what state to enter

Despite its simplicity, the Turing machine can simulate any computer algorithm, making it **Turing-complete**.

### Church-Turing Thesis (1936)

Independently of Alonzo Church, Turing proved that the Entscheidungsproblem (decision problem) was undecidable. He then proved that his Turing machines and Church's lambda calculus define **exactly the same class** of computable functions.

This led to the Church-Turing thesis: any function that can be computed by any mechanical process can be computed by a Turing machine (or equivalently, expressed in lambda calculus).

### The Turing Test (1950)

In his paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," Turing proposed a test of a machine's ability to exhibit intelligent behaviour equivalent to, or indistinguishable from, that of a human. The test involves a human evaluator who converses with a human and a machine without knowing which is which. If the evaluator cannot reliably tell the machine from the human, the machine is said to have passed the test.

### Codebreaking at Bletchley Park (1939-1945)

Turing designed the bombe, an electromechanical device used to help decipher German Enigma-encrypted messages. His work was estimated to have shortened the war in Europe by more than two years and saved over 14 million lives.

## Influence

### Influenced by

- **Alonzo Church** — Turing's doctoral supervisor; proved equivalence of their models
- **David Hilbert** — Entscheidungsproblem that Turing set out to solve
- **Kurt Gödel** — Incompleteness theorems that set the stage for computability theory

### Influenced

- **John von Neumann** — Von Neumann architecture influenced by Turing's stored-program concept
- **All of modern computing** — Every computer is a Turing machine; the Church-Turing thesis defines computation
- **Artificial Intelligence** — Turing Test established the field's foundational question
- **Computer Science** — Turing established the theoretical foundations of the entire discipline
